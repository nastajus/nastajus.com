Good afternoon Ian,

 

Vergence: 

Divergence - eyes moving apart to focus on a far target

Convergence - eyes moving closer together at a near target

The focusing system, accommodation, can drive vergence, and the eye's focusing muscles can be forced to work harder in order to bring the eyes to a more converged state.  This effort can be very fatiguing with computer work and the reading process. 

 

Please do the following if possible every day, once a day.  If symptoms ramp up to >5/10, please dial it back.  

 

Monocular clock peripheral awareness: checks for tunneling of vision

1. Arrange small sticky note pieces or painter's tape in a clock formation, about 80 cm in diameter on a broad expanse of wall, wide enough to reach the max of your peripheral vision in all directions.  Do not place a sticker in the 3 and 9 o'clock positions (natural blind spot).  Place a small sticker or post-it note in the center at eye level.  Stand a few feet away from the clock.  
2. Cover one eye with your hand.
3. Look at the center sticker with one eye. Make sure you can see all clock dots simultaneously in your side vision.  If you are having trouble, you can try tapping your feet or shoulder to see if this helps.  With practice you should be able to see the all dots at the same time equally with each eye, without flickering or disappearing of any of the dots.  

Clock/stick activity (cover one eye; can use a pencil with eraser on the end):

Stand in front of the clock where the stick can touch the wall without leaning forward.  Hold the stick and place it on the center dot. 
Look at the center dot.  Keep looking at the center and in your side vision, locate one of the clock dots.  While fixating at center, move the stick to the dot.  Now look at where you moved the stick with your eyes and head and make any corrections.  Then as you keep the stick there, look back to center (stick doesn't move back to center).  Now locate a new random dot in your side vision.  Move the stick to that new location while fixating on the center sticker.  Do for a couple of minutes with each eye.  Then recheck the clock to see if more dots are visible.

If too difficult, make the clock smaller to begin with and expand out as you get better!  

Bean bag arc throws - co-ordination, balance, and peripheral guided activity: 

Throw a hand-sized bean bag (rolled up sock or glove) in an arc over your head from one hand to the other.  Use your peripheral vision while looking straight ahead.  Try doing this while walking heel to toe, forwards and backwards, for a couple of minutes in a safe area.

Smooth eye tracking:

 

Sit down on a stable chair.  Hold a pen tip at your eye level at about arm's length, and slowly move it to one side, tracking with your eyes and head is still.  Always be aware of the background room at the same time!  Follow the pen to the right, left, up, and down, 3x each direction, closing eyes after each track to bring the pen back to center.  Don't go too fast with the pen (should take a good 5 seconds in each direction).  Add large diagonal X's to work on diagonal eye movements as well.  

 

Step-together activity - trains motor planning and communication between right and left sides of the brain: 

Step forward with one foot.  Move the opposite arm forward.

Feet together and arms down at sides.

Step forward with the other foot while moving the opposite arm forward.

Feet together and arms down at sides.

10 steps forward, and 10 steps backward for a few minutes.  When you step backward with your foot, opposite arm goes forward.  

Remember not to look at the floor, but look straight ahead while you do this.
